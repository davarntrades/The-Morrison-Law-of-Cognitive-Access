## What “They Won’t See It” Does

“They won’t see it” is a moral diagnosis. It places the failure inside the person — in their character, their choices, their willingness.

```
"They won't see it" implies:

  The truth is available.
  The path to it is open.
  They have chosen not to walk it.

  The failure is volitional.
  The problem is their refusal.
  The solution is to overcome that refusal.

  Methods attempted:
    Argue harder.
    Present more evidence.
    Apply social pressure.
    Appeal to emotion.
    Repeat. Louder.
    Give up.
    Declare bad faith.
    Assign moral failure.
```

Every one of these responses follows logically from the diagnosis. If the failure is refusal, then overcoming refusal is the solution. Apply pressure until the refusal breaks. Or conclude that the person is not acting in good faith and disengage.

```
The outcomes of this diagnosis:

  Relationships damaged.
  Communication closed.
  Judgment issued.
  Loop sealed.
  Understanding: no closer.

  The diagnosis made the problem unsolvable
  by misidentifying what the problem is.
```

## What “They Can’t See It Yet” Does

“They can’t see it yet” is a geometric diagnosis. It places the failure in the structure — in the topology of the reachable set, not in the character of the person.

```
"They can't see it yet" implies:

  T ∉ Reach(X).

  The truth exists.
  The path to it does not yet exist
  in this person's geometry.

  The failure is structural.
  The problem is geometric.
  The solution is topological expansion.

  Not: overcome their refusal.
  But: what structural event would build
       the geometry required for T to land?

  Methods that follow from this diagnosis:
    What does Reach(X) currently contain?
    What is the closest state to T
    that is inside Reach(X)?
    What experience would expand
    the manifold toward T?
    What structural event —
    not what argument —
    would build the missing geometry?
```

```
The outcomes of this diagnosis:

  Curiosity instead of judgment.
  A solvable problem instead of a moral failure.
  A question with engineering answers
  instead of a question with no reliable answer.
  Understanding: now possible.

  The diagnosis made the problem solvable
  by identifying what the problem actually is.
```

## Why This Is the Most Useful Aspect of the Law

Every field that has tried to solve “why people cannot see the truth” has treated it as a moral problem.

```
Psychology:    cognitive bias. motivated reasoning.
               The person is protecting their ego.
               Solution: make them feel safe enough
               to lower their defences.

Education:     insufficient knowledge.
               The person has not been taught properly.
               Solution: better teaching. more repetition.

Persuasion:    insufficient evidence or framing.
               The person has not been convinced.
               Solution: better argument. stronger evidence.

Diplomacy:     bad faith. strategic obstruction.
               The person will not cooperate.
               Solution: incentives. pressure. negotiation.

Theology:      sin. spiritual blindness. hardness of heart.
               The person has turned from the truth.
               Solution: conversion. repentance. grace.
```

Every one of these diagnoses has produced solutions that work sometimes and fail most of the time. Because they are all aimed at the wrong layer.

```
They are all L-axis interventions
aimed at a C-axis problem.

C ⊥ L.

Argument is L-axis.
Evidence is L-axis.
Emotional appeal is L-axis.
Social pressure is L-axis.
Moral condemnation is L-axis.

The topology of the reachable set
is C-axis.

L-axis interventions do not reach
the C-axis problem.
The axes are orthogonal.
They do not meet.

∂C/∂I ≈ 0.

The structural topology does not update
from information input alone.
No L-axis input, however perfect,
expands Reach(X) reliably.

All of the methods listed above
are pouring information
into a structure that cannot update from it.
And then attributing the failure to update
to a flaw in the person.

The Morrison Law says:
There is no flaw in the person.
There is a geometric condition.
T ∉ Reach(X).

And geometric conditions
require geometric solutions.
```

## The Deepest Implication

The Morrison Law does not just change what we do when understanding fails. It changes what understanding failure means.

```
BEFORE THE LAW:
  Understanding failure = someone's fault.
  The person who cannot see:
    is biased, afraid, stubborn,
    self-interested, or acting in bad faith.

  The person who cannot make them see:
    explained poorly, argued weakly,
    chose the wrong evidence,
    failed to connect emotionally.

  Fault. Failure. Moral assessment.
  Always someone to blame.
  Never a solution that works reliably.

AFTER THE LAW:
  Understanding failure = geometric condition.
  T ∉ Reach(X).

  No fault.
  No failure.
  No moral assessment required.

  A structural observation:
  the topology does not yet contain
  the states required to receive this truth.

  Followed by an engineering question:
  what builds the topology that does?

  Fault produces judgment.
  Geometry produces questions.
  Questions produce solutions.
  Solutions produce understanding.

  This is the conversion.
  This is what changes.
  This is why it matters.
```
