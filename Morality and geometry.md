## What the Law Removes

```
The Morrison Law removes moral fault
from the act of not understanding.
```

This is the precise removal. Not morality from human life. Not responsibility from human action. Moral fault — specifically — from the initial act of not being able to see a truth.

**Before the law:**

```
"They won't see it."

Implies:
  The truth is available.
  The path to it is open.
  They have chosen not to walk it.
  The failure is volitional.
  Character is implicated.
  Moral verdict: guilty of wilful blindness.

Consequences of this verdict:
  Judgment issued.
  Relationship damaged.
  Solution: pressure, shame, condemnation.
  Outcome: defensive hardening.
  Understanding: no closer.
  Often: further away.
```

**After the law:**

```
T ∉ Reach(X).

States:
  The truth exists.
  The path to it does not yet exist
  in this person's geometric structure.
  The failure is topological.
  Character is not implicated.
  No moral verdict required or appropriate.

Consequences of this observation:
  No judgment issued.
  Relationship preserved.
  Solution: what structural event
            builds the missing geometry?
  Outcome: expansion possible.
  Understanding: now a solvable problem.
```

The removal is specific and complete. The person who cannot see a truth is not morally failing. They are in a geometric condition. The topology does not contain the path.

```
The analogy is exact:

A person who cannot see ultraviolet light
is not morally failing.
The topology of their visual system
does not contain those states.
No fault. No choice. Geometry.

A person whose cognitive manifold
does not contain the states required
to see a particular truth
is in the same condition.
No fault. No choice. Geometry.

T ∉ Reach(X).
That is all.
```

## What the Law Does Not Remove

```
The Morrison Law does not remove
moral responsibility from what you do
with the limitation
once you become aware of it.
```

This is the critical distinction. The law removes fault from blindness. It does not remove responsibility from wilful closure.

```
T ∉ Reach(X) explains:
  Why the person cannot see the truth now.
  The initial failure to understand.
  The genuine inability to receive T.

T ∉ Reach(X) does not excuse:
  The deliberate refusal to expand.
  The active hardening of the basin.
  The choice to defend closure
  once the limitation is named.
  The weaponisation of geometric language
  to avoid accountability.
```

Once a person becomes aware that their topology may not contain the states required to see a truth — they have a choice. Not to see the truth immediately. They still cannot do that. The geometry does not change by being named. But they can choose to create the conditions under which their topology expands.

```
dI/dt > 0 is available as a choice.

Remaining open to structural events.
Entering genuinely unfamiliar territory.
Tolerating the discomfort of expansion.
Not hardening the basin deliberately.
Not actively defending the closure.
Not using "T ∉ Reach(X)"
as a shield against accountability.

These are choices.
They are moral choices.
The law does not remove them.
```
